What exactly is a Food Desert?

The phrase Food Deserts describes a phenomenon where, due to several factors, there is a lack of immediately available fresh food sources to communities across the United States. Fresh food sources can be defined as fresh fruits, vegetables, grains, dairy products, and meat all of which are necessary to make up a full and healthy diet that can be prepared in the home and not eaten on the go. Many Americans living in rural, urban, or low-income areas are stranded in food deserts and may be unable to access affordable, healthy foods, leaving their diets lacking essential nutrients.

What are Food Deserts?

The United States Department of Agriculture has defined food deserts as geographic areas where a person must travel more than half a mile to reach a supermarket in an urban area or more than ten miles in a rural area. Rural, urban, and impoverished areas are more like to coincide with areas designated as food deserts. Where these places lack access to fresh food sources there is instead a high incidence of fast food franchises and convenience stores.


Who's at risk?

This limited access can be directly correlated to the median income of the community. To put it succinctly: poorer communities have greater difficulties accessing sources for fresh food.
